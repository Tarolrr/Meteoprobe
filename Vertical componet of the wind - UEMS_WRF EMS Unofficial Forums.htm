            meteoadriatic wrote:It is vertical but...

            did you properly calculated speed from Pascal/second? On low levels, take something like 100 Pascals for every 8 meters of height... so for 1 meter of height difference you have around 12.5 Pascals of pressure difference. That gives, the speed of 1m/s would equal 12.5Pa/s if my calculation is correct. What ratio you used?
            I have used the hydrostatic equation:

            [1] dp/dz=-d*g*dt/dz

            where
            omega=dp/dt is omega vertical velocity (Pa/s)
            d is the air density (km/m^3)
            g is the gravity acceleration (m/s^2)
            dz/dt is the (geometric) vertical velocity (m/s)

            Obviously I have reversed the formula to calculate dz/dt.

            I have calculated air density using the formula:

            [2] d=pa/(287.05*T)+pv/(461.495*T)

            where
            pa is the partial pressur of the dry air (Pa)
            pv is the partial pressure of the water vapor (Pa)
            T is the air temperatur (K)

            I have calculated pv with the formula

            [3] pv=RH/100*pvsat (Pa)

            where
            RH is the air relative humidity (%)
            pvsat is the partial pressure of the water vapor @ saturation (Pa)

            I have finally calculated pvsat with the formula

            [4] pvsat=601.78*10^((7.5*T-2048.625)/(T-35.85)) (Pa)

            Air density near the ground is something about 1.2 kg/m^3, g=9.8 m/s^2, so vertical velocity calculated with [1] is about omega/12, according to your value meteoadriatic

            Example omega = 30Pa/s => vertical velocity = -2.6 m/s (descending)

            I found in the GRIB file the field wdnmxsfc (wupmxsfc) surface Maximum downward (upward) vertical motion between output times (m/s).
            I think it is the vertical velocity near the ground level.

            I will investigate comparing the calculated vertical velocity with this WRF field.

            It is possibile that the grid resolution underestimate a lot the vertical velocity values along the steep slopes of the mountains?

              oib wrote:I will investigate comparing the calculated vertical velocity with this WRF field.
              I have plotted for comparison:
              + vertical velocity interpolated to 300 m above ground (calculated from GRIB field vvelprs)
              + vertical velocity interpolated to 2 m above ground (calculated from GRIB field vvelprs)
              + wupmxsfc (GRIB field "surface Maximum downward vertical motion between output times")

              The three grid have very very very similar shape.

              wupmxsfc is always grater than vertical velocity calculated from omega (around 1.5 times greater). I expected this because wupmxsfc is the maximum value between output times, instead the other two are instantaneous values.

              Calculated vertical velocity @ 300 m is very close to vertical velocity @ 2 m.

              My conclusions are that:
              + the equations I have used are right
              + for fast computation, vertical velocity (m/s) near ground level is roughly equal to -omega/12.5 (Pa/s)
              + vertical motion @ ground level are slightly greater than vertical motion @ 300 m above the ground (orografic forcing, I think)
